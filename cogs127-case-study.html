COGS 127 Project

Extensions to WhatsApp

Helping middle-aged and older immigrant adults translate messages with more confidence inside an app they already use.

UC San Diego COGS 127 WhatsApp Prototype User Testing Accessibility

Role

Design researcher and prototype evaluator

I helped connect interview insights and user testing feedback to clearer translation interface decisions.

Methods

Interviews, user flows, and prototype testing

Our team studied communication pain points, tested three prototype directions, and iterated on the strongest flow.

Outcome

More visible in-app translation support

The final direction emphasized direct translation access, larger controls, clearer labels, and less app switching.

Overview

Why this project mattered

Many messaging apps assume users can easily recognize interface patterns, understand English labels, and move between translation tools without stress. For middle-aged and older immigrant adults with limited digital literacy, that can turn a simple message into a frustrating or risky task. Our project explored how WhatsApp could make translation support more visible, integrated, and confidence-building.

Problem Statement

The user need

Language barriers and limited digital literacy make it difficult for middle-aged and older immigrant adults to confidently communicate through messaging platforms such as WhatsApp. Users may struggle to understand English messages, respond to businesses or service providers, and navigate translation features, often causing frustration, dependence on family members, or avoidance of important communication altogether.

Target Users

Who we designed for

Middle-aged adults Older adults Immigrant communities English as a second language users People with limited technology experience

Research Findings

What we learned before redesigning

01

Translation was fragmented

Participants often moved between WhatsApp and Google Translate, which added extra steps and cognitive load.

02

Hidden controls went unused

Existing translation options were often overlooked because they were buried behind menus or unclear icons.

03

Fear changed behavior

Some users avoided opening or acting on messages because they could not easily judge whether messages were safe.

Design Response

What the prototype needed to support

Integrated translation

Translation should happen inside the messaging flow instead of forcing users to switch apps.

Visible controls

Translation buttons should be easy to notice, clearly labeled, and available near the moment of need.

Guided confidence

The interface should help users understand what happened after translation and reduce hesitation.

Prototype Testing

Why Prototype B became the strongest direction

Prototype A

Structured but slower

Some users found it familiar and organized, but it required extra steps before the flow became smooth.

Prototype B

Fastest and easiest to understand

Users liked seeing the translation option directly near the message because it reduced searching and felt more natural.

Prototype C

Least intuitive

The toggle was harder to notice, and users were unsure whether translation was on or off.

Before and After 01

Translating received texts

During user testing, participants often had trouble finding the translation feature because the original button was small and easy to miss. The redesign makes the translation action more noticeable by increasing the button size and adding a clearer label beside the received message.

Before and After 02

Translating written texts

Participants reported difficulty selecting the right control because the buttons were placed too close together. The updated design adds more spacing around the controls and makes the language-change area more prominent, helping reduce accidental selections.

Before and After 03

Translate language menu

Users wanted bigger buttons and a clearer way to select specific translation functions. By stacking the language options vertically, the redesigned menu creates larger selection areas and makes the options easier to access for users with less precise motor control.

Reflection

What I learned

The biggest takeaway from this user testing process is that small UI details can create major barriers. A feature can be useful, but if users cannot find it quickly or select it comfortably, the design still needs work. This project helped me understand how visibility, cognitive load, recognition over recall, and trust all shape whether users feel confident enough to communicate independently.

Next Steps

How I would keep improving it

If I continued this project, I would run another round of testing with the redesigned screens to see whether users can find translation faster, select language controls with fewer mistakes, and complete the task without extra guidance. I would also explore voice message translation, automatic language detection, and clearer first-time labels for users who are new to translation tools.
